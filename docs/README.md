# OctoAcme Project Management Playbook

## Purpose
This documentation exists to give OctoAcme teams a shared, practical operating model for delivering projects from idea to outcomes. Use this README as the starting point to understand the process, find role-specific guidance, and jump to phase-specific templates and checklists.

## OctoAcme Process Overview
OctoAcme uses a five-phase lifecycle to keep delivery structured and predictable: **Initiation**, **Planning**, **Execution**, **Release**, and **Retrospective**. Each phase has explicit outputs so teams can move forward with clarity: a project one-pager and stakeholder alignment in initiation, prioritized backlog and Definition of Done in planning, tracked execution and risk updates during delivery, release notes and rollback planning before deployment, and documented action items after retrospectives.

Accountability is distributed across clearly defined roles. **Project Managers** coordinate timelines, dependencies, risks, and communications. **Product Managers** define goals, prioritize work, and track customer and business outcomes. **Developers** build and test solutions while surfacing technical risks. **QA/Testing** validates acceptance criteria and release readiness through planned and exploratory quality checks.

Communication and risk management are built into the team rhythm. Daily standups and weekly delivery syncs surface blockers early, while regular stakeholder updates maintain alignment on scope, milestones, and decisions. Risks are captured in a register, assessed by impact and likelihood, assigned owners, and reviewed continuously with clear escalation paths from team-level triage to sponsor-level escalation for business-critical issues.

Quality and delivery are enforced through checkpoints before and during release. Teams are expected to use CI for tests and security scanning, follow pull request review conventions, and verify Definition of Done compliance before closing work. Release readiness requires passing quality gates, smoke-test validation, and rollback preparedness, and every retrospective feeds concrete improvements back into future planning cycles.
